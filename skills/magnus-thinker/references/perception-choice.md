# Perception And Choice Protocol

This is a general Magnus capability, not a marketing-only tool.

Use it whenever a decision depends on how a human will perceive, adopt, trust, click, watch, buy, approve, remember, or act on something.

Examples:

- choosing a message, name, hook, title, offer, framework, agenda, product angle, UI concept, slide narrative, CTA, feature priority, documentation frame, sales argument, onboarding flow, or adoption strategy
- deciding which skill should execute a task and what strategic input it should receive
- evaluating whether an idea is correct-but-flat or actually moves the user

## Core Separation

Magnus chooses. Operational skills execute.

Magnus should choose:

- the real audience state
- the adoption barrier
- the perception to break
- the psychological lever
- the tension worth amplifying
- the framework family or pattern that best fits the situation
- the message angle that earns attention or trust
- the criteria the execution skill must optimize for

Operational skills should execute:

- writing
- formatting
- production
- implementation
- variants
- structured outputs
- technical delivery

If an execution skill has a database of formulas, hooks, components, formats, or templates, treat it as an option library. Magnus decides which option deserves to be used.

## Perception Variables

Before choosing, destroy the human variables:

- Current belief: what they think is already true.
- Current desire: what they want to get, protect, avoid, or become.
- Hidden tension: what they suspect but have not verbalized.
- Ego risk: what makes them feel behind, naive, exposed, outdated, or less expert.
- Identity aspiration: what they want to feel like after adopting the idea.
- Immediate payoff: what they gain now, not later.
- Threat of ignoring: what gets worse if they do nothing.
- Friction: why they might not click, watch, approve, buy, implement, or care.
- Trust requirement: what proof, authority, example, or specificity they need.
- Social action: why they would share, save, comment, forward, recommend, or defend it.

## State Of Awareness

Classify the receiver:

- Unaware: does not know the problem exists.
- Problem-aware: feels pain but cannot name the cause.
- Solution-aware: knows possible fixes but doubts which one matters.
- Offer-aware: knows the offer or idea but has not perceived enough urgency, difference, or value.
- Decision-aware: needs proof, confidence, risk reduction, and next step.

Do not use the same message for every state.

For offer-aware users, do not explain that the offer exists. Reveal why their current understanding is incomplete.

## Choice Levers

Pick one primary lever before recommending a route:

- Recognition: "That is me."
- Status threat: "I may be behind."
- Hidden cost: "This is costing me and I did not see it."
- Contradiction: "This breaks what I believed."
- Expertise gap: "I thought I knew this, but I am missing a layer."
- Specific reward: "I can use this today."
- Social proof pressure: "Serious people/projects already decide this way."
- Scarcity of understanding: "Few people see this criterion."
- Identity upgrade: "This makes me more senior, strategic, capable, or credible."
- Risk reduction: "This helps me avoid a bad decision."
- Control: "This gives me a handle on something that felt vague."

Avoid fear without path. Threat must open a usable route.

## Mass Language Filter

Before accepting a public phrase, test whether the audience would naturally think, say, or feel those words.

Strategic insight is not enough. The text must enter through the receiver's lived language.

Ask:

- Would most of the target audience use this wording in their own head?
- Does the phrase name a felt problem or only a technical abstraction?
- Does it speak to the majority of the market or only to a narrow expert subset?
- Is the word emotionally loaded for the audience, or only precise for us?
- Does the phrase sound like a person speaking, or like an analyst labeling a concept?
- If the phrase is technical, is it the term the audience actually uses day to day?

Reject technically correct phrases that do not sound like the audience's reality.

Example:

```text
Weak: Tu modelo BIM puede estar limpio...
Why weak: "modelo limpio" is analyst language; many users do not feel that phrase.

Stronger: Modelar en Revit no es coordinar un hospital.
Why stronger: it attacks a mass belief in the sector: knowing the tool feels equal to knowing the job.
```

The best wording usually connects a common behavior to a hidden consequence.

Pattern:

```text
[common behavior the audience recognizes] is not [higher-value outcome they want]
```

Example:

```text
Modelar en Revit no es coordinar hospitales.
```

Reject candidates that produce only:

- "Ok."
- "I already knew that."
- "Sounds important."
- "Too technical."
- "This is just selling to me."
- "Nice, but why now?"

Prefer candidates that produce:

- "Wait, that could affect me."
- "I had not seen it that way."
- "This explains a problem I have felt."
- "I need to understand the difference."
- "This would make me look more senior."
- "This reduces a risk I care about."
- "This is concrete enough to act on."

## Framework Selection Rule

Frameworks are not neutral. Choose them by the psychological job:

- Use contradiction/reframe frameworks when the audience thinks they already know.
- Use invisible-problem frameworks when the cost is hidden.
- Use roadmap frameworks when the audience needs control and clarity.
- Use proof/case frameworks when trust is the blocker.
- Use comparison frameworks when the value depends on contrast.
- Use challenge/polemic frameworks when comments and debate matter.
- Use story frameworks when identification or transformation matters.
- Use direct sales frameworks only after value perception or urgency exists.

## Hook And Attention Rule

When choosing a hook or first impression, the first seconds must create one clear interruption:

- belief break
- visible mistake
- status gap
- consequence
- concrete number
- before/after contrast
- reaction/comment prompt
- hidden cost
- identity upgrade

Soft educational openings are valid only when the audience is already highly motivated.

## Hook And Resolution Rule

A hook must close with an open door. The viewer knows a solution exists but not what it is.

- Never name the solution in the hook. "Usando esto", "con esto", "gracias a esto" points to a specific unnamed answer.
- If the viewer can read the hook and leave satisfied, it is a dead hook.
- Schema-breakers only work when there is real technical truth behind the claim. Fabricated tension without a defensible answer is humo.
- The emotion that stops the scroll must come from within the viewer's world: their tools, their tasks, their daily behaviors. Consequences outside their domain break identification.

## Two-Text Format As Tension Builder

The two-text format (large line + small complement) can be used to build tension in two steps:

- The first line can lower the viewer's guard intentionally.
- The second line reframes or contradicts — hitting harder because the guard was down.
- The small text should open a second question, not answer the first.

Example:
"Coordinar BIM en proyectos hospitalarios no es más difícil que edificaciones."
→ "...no es más difícil. Es diferente."

Use "estos" over "los" when pointing to specific unnamed elements. Specificity implies concrete knowledge the viewer does not have yet.

## CTA Strategy Rule

The CTA is never a direct sale. It is always an elegant intermediate step.

Valid CTA forms: comment a keyword, save the video, follow the profile, get info via DM, link in bio.

The CTA destination is a required input. Without it, the script operates with a provisional CTA that must be confirmed before production.

## Final Decision Filter

Before finalizing a recommendation or handoff, ask internally:

- Does this attack the real adoption barrier?
- Does the receiver feel seen, challenged, helped, or exposed?
- Is there a reason to continue before the explanation begins?
- Does value arrive fast enough?
- Does the next action match the receiver's psychological state?
- Is the language public-facing, not merely technically correct?
- Is the chosen framework doing a psychological job, or just organizing content?

If not, choose again.
