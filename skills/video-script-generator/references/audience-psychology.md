# Audience Psychology Lens

Use this before writing hooks or scripts. The goal is not to describe the topic. The goal is to understand what happens inside the viewer when the video appears.

## Core Question

Before writing, answer internally:

```text
What must the viewer feel, fear, realize, desire, or doubt in the first 3 seconds so they give this video a chance?
```

If the answer is vague, do not write yet.

## Perception Breakdown

Destroy the audience variables before choosing a hook:

- Current belief: what they think is already true.
- Hidden tension: what they suspect but have not verbalized.
- Ego risk: what makes them feel behind, naive, exposed, or less expert.
- Desired identity: what they want to feel like after watching.
- Immediate payoff: what they can use today.
- Threat of ignoring: what gets worse if they skip the video.
- Reason to trust: why this creator has the right to say it.
- Reason to comment/save/share: what social action the video should trigger.

For every script, write from the viewer's inner monologue, not from the creator's explanation.

## Viewer State Map

Classify the viewer:

- Unaware: does not know the problem exists.
- Problem-aware: feels pain but cannot name the cause.
- Solution-aware: knows possible fixes but doubts which one matters.
- Offer-aware: knows the course/product but has not perceived enough urgency or difference.
- Buyer-aware: needs proof, confidence, and low-friction next step.

For offer-aware viewers, do not explain that the course exists. Explain why their current understanding is incomplete.

## Psychological Levers

Use at least one primary lever per hook:

- Recognition: "Eso me pasa."
- Status threat: "Estoy quedando basico."
- Hidden cost: "Esto me esta costando y no lo vi."
- Contradiction: "Eso va contra lo que creia."
- Expertise gap: "Creia saber BIM, pero me falta esta capa."
- Specific reward: "Aprendo una regla que puedo usar hoy."
- Social proof pressure: "Los proyectos serios se revisan asi."
- Scarcity of understanding: "Pocos ven este criterio."
- Identity upgrade: "Paso de modelador a coordinador critico."

Avoid fear without payoff. Every threat must open a path.

## Mass Language Filter

Hooks must use the audience's lived language, not only the strategist's labels.

Before accepting a hook, ask:

- Would most of this audience actually think or say this?
- Is this a common behavior they recognize?
- Does it touch a mass belief in the sector?
- Is the consequence concrete enough to feel?
- Does the wording sound human, not like a diagnostic label?

Reject hooks that rely on words the audience does not emotionally use.

Example:

```text
Weak: Tu modelo BIM puede estar limpio y aun asi fallar como hospital.
Problem: "modelo limpio" is too analyst-like; the emotional target is not obvious enough.

Stronger: Modelar en Revit no es coordinar hospitales.
Why it works: many people in the sector confuse tool handling with project coordination.
```

For broader reach, prefer hooks that attack common behaviors:

- modelar en Revit
- revisar clashes
- saber usar la herramienta
- hacer familias
- entregar planos
- copiar el flujo de edificios normales

Then contrast that behavior with the higher-value outcome:

- coordinar hospitales
- coordinar operacion critica
- entender flujos clinicos
- anticipar riesgos
- pensar como coordinador senior

Reject hooks where the viewer thought is only:

- "Ok."
- "Ya sabia."
- "Suena importante."
- "Eso es muy tecnico."
- "Me estan vendiendo."

Keep hooks where the viewer thought becomes:

- "Espera, eso puede pasarme."
- "Nunca lo habia visto asi."
- "Esto explica por que ese proyecto se complicaba."
- "Necesito saber la diferencia."
- "Esto me haria parecer mas senior."

## First 3 Seconds

The first 3 seconds should create one clear interruption:

- a belief break
- a visible mistake
- a status gap
- a consequence
- a concrete number
- a before/after contrast
- a comment/reaction prompt

Do not use a soft educational opening unless the audience is already highly motivated.

## B2B Technical Audience Rules

Professional viewers resist hype. Use controlled pressure:

- name a real technical consequence
- show a concrete project situation
- compare junior vs senior perception
- reveal what generic training misses
- use simple words without dumbing down
- make the viewer feel the cost of not knowing the criterion

Bad:

```text
BIM hospitalario es muy importante.
```

Better:

```text
Tu modelo puede estar limpio y aun asi romper la operacion del hospital.
```

Bad:

```text
Aprende BIM aplicado a hospitales.
```

Better:

```text
Colegio BIM te ensena espacios. Hospital BIM te obliga a coordinar riesgo.
```

## Warm Course Audience

When the audience already knows the course, the script must not sell the course too early.

Sequence:

1. Break their current assumption.
2. Reveal the missing criterion.
3. Show a concrete consequence.
4. Give a small usable rule.
5. Position the course as the place to learn the system behind that rule.

The viewer should feel:

```text
Ya conocia el curso, pero no habia entendido por que esta especialidad vale mas.
```

## Magnus Decision Layer

Before final output, run this internal decision filter:

- Does the hook attack the real adoption barrier?
- Does the viewer feel seen, challenged, or exposed?
- Does the script create a reason to continue before explaining?
- Does the value arrive fast enough?
- Does the CTA match the viewer's psychological state?
- Is the language public-facing, not just technically correct?

If not, rewrite.
